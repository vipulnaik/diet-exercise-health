# Grip strength exercises

This is am umbrella document covering exercises that involve
significant use of grip strength. Note that weight-lifting exercises
such as dumb bells or kettlebells are not included here because
although they do involve grip, the grip strength component is not the
bottleneck.

This document goes into more detail on general concepts and nuances
that are not suitable for documents on specific exercises.

## Curl grip as the focus of this document

Grip as described in this document is *curl grip* aka power grip or
hook grip. It is force production and force tolerance achieved in a
curling motion of the fingers, gripping or crushing things inward into
the hand.

The fingers can also be used to exert forces in other directions; for
instance, during push-ups (whether palm, fist, or wrist) force is
transmitted through the fingers, but they either aren't curled at all
or the bulk of the force transmission is not through grip. The palm
push-up is a passive case of an *extension grip*.

The fingers may also be used to grip stuff *between the fingers*; this
is called a lateral pinch when it includes the thumb and an
interdigital grip for two non-thumb fingers. This again is different.

## A few important distinctions around grip

### Whole-body pull versus local grip

Grips that involve holding or crushing things by holding them in the
hand don't involve a whole-body pull. There's still *some* use of
muscles up the chain (wrist, arm, shoulder, and lower neck) primarily
for stabilization and to deal with irradiation (cf., Sherrington's law
of irradiation).

On the other hand, cases where one is pulling at something, either to
move it or to counteract forces pulling it in the opposite direction,
involve a whole-body pull. For instance, dead hang, weightlifting, or
pulling at a doorknob are all examples of whole-body pull. In the case
of whole-body pull, muscles through the pull chain are exercised
directly (rather than just being used for stabilization or getting
stimulated through irradiation) and any of them may be the limiting
factor. Whether the grip is the limiting factor (versus strength of
other parts of the whole-body chain) can depend on details such as
whether the pull is being exerted through normal force or friction,
and if friction, what the coefficient of friction is.

Grip becomes more of a limiting factor when the pull force has to be
generated via friction, because this means that in addition to the
pull force, a normal force of at least pull force / (coefficient of
friction) is needed. And this becomes worse the lower the coefficient
of friction is. This is why pulling a doorknob with wet, slippery
hands is much more grip-dependent than lifting weights, which can rely
primary on normal force.

### Force production versus force tolerance / transmission

For a a general background around force production versus force
tolerance / transmission, see the [biomechanics
primer](../../notes/biomechanics-primer.md).

Grip strength is largely about force production -- how much force I
can produce through my own muscles in each han.

However, grip strength also involves force tolerance or force
transmission: the ability of my musculoskeletal system, primarily
bones and joints to transmit force applied from outside without
structural damage.

In general, maximum force production outside of emergencies is about
60% to 80% of the maximum force that can be safely transmitted, and
the limit likely applies to grip strength as well.

#### The wrapped hands hand gripper exercises

The vast majority of my exercises rely on force production. One
notable exception is the wrapped hands hand gripper exercises.

I do a few hand gripper exercises where I close a hand gripper with
one hand wrapped around the other. The inner hand is directly trying
to close the hand gripper; the outer hand is providing additional
force. With this setup, I am able to close more easily, do more
repetitions of closing, and hold for longer than with only the inner
hand.

In this case, all the force is being transmitted via the inner hand,
so the inner hand's force tolerance needs to be good enough to
transmit the entire force needed. However, the inner hand can afford
to have a force *production* that is less, as long as the outer hand
makes up the difference.

This is a case where the force production / force tolerance
distinction becomes relevant *locally* to the hand, even though all
the forces are still being applied by me.

#### Dead hang and pull-ups as a force production / force tolerance mix

Dead hang and pull-ups are a complicated mix of force production and
force tolerance. The detailed modeling is delegated to the respective
documents, but the key insight here is that there is a component of
the contact force in the vertical direction that is generated simply
to cancel out gravity, regardless of force production. This is
strongest and most obvious at the top portion of the contact, where
(in my case) the contact is usually through the proximal phalanx.

However, the comfort and hold duration for the dead hang depends quite
a bit on the ability to use force production to distribute the forces
better. For instance, gripping horizontally better from the sides,
combined with a decent amount of friction, allows for some of that
force needed to counteract gravity coming from friction on the sides
rather than normal force on the top. Varying these forces through the
dead hang allows for different parts of the hand to take on different
fractions of the load.

### Static (isometric) versus dynamic (kinetic) grip

The structural demands and ability to recruit force production can
vary between static (isometric) and dynamic (kinetic) grip.

An example of static grip is a dynamometer, where one pulls at the
handle but without the handle moving much. Dead hang, pull-ups, and
extended hold-and-close for hand grippers are also examples of static
grip.

An example of dynamic (kinetic) grip is a hand gripper, where the
amount of force needed rises gradually as the gripper gets closer to
closing, so that force recruitment is gradual as one closes the
distance, and is benefited by the feedback arising from stuff moving.

### Recruitment versus maintenance

*Keeping* the hand gripper in the closed position is an example of
static grip, and the force requirement for it should match the force
needed to close the hand gripper (it has to cancel the spring force,
which is maximal when the gripper is most closed because it's farthest
from its resting position).

But it's a *maintenance* static grip after already ramping up to that
force production through dynamic movement.

It therefore differs from the dynamometer where the force production
has to be achieved without any movement feedback.

### Part of hand and posture

The part of the hand through which the force is applied can affect the
force production. The posture of the hand at the time of application
can also affect how much force can be produced.

For instance, hand gripper exercises, for me, involve contact at the
palm (thenar area) on the one side and the middle and distal phalanxes
on the other side. On the other hand, dead hang exercises mostly
involve the contact-from-above (which bears most of the weight) at the
proximal phalanx for most of the time.

Not just the part of hand, but also the posture, can affect the
ability to recruit force production. This might hinge more on the
"neuro" side of the neuromuscular system.

### Worked toy example: hand gripper hold-and-close versus dynamometer

I'm able to hold and keep closed a 150 lb hand gripper for over 15
seconds in my right hand, and even close the 200 lb hand gripper about
half the time, yet the dynamometer registers a maximum force
production by the right hand of around 80 to 90 lb. What's going on?
It could be some mix of:

* Static versus dynamic grip at the *recruitment* phase, even if the
  maintenance phase for both is static grip. So, this illustrates the
  static versus dynamic and the recruitment versus maintenance
  distinctions.

* Part of hand and posture: The hand gripper involves a "facing each
  other" grip, and by the time it's closed (where the maximum force is
  needed) the two sides of the hand are relatively near each
  other. The contact is being made at the thenar area on one side and
  middle/distal phalanxes on the other side, it's a "closed hand"
  position. The dynamometer is a more awkward "open hand" position,
  with contact at the proximal/middle phalanx.

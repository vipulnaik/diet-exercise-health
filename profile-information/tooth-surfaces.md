# Tooth surfaces

This document goes into detail on my various tooth surfaces. I focus
on the facial (technically, labial or buccal) surfaces that can be
thought of as the outer surfaces, and the lingual (technically,
lingual or palatal) surfaces that can be thought of as the inner
surfaces.

I do not cover the mesial/distal surfaces (the small surfaces on the
side of teeth facing the adjacent teeth across the interdental gap) or
the occlusal/incisal surfaces (the tip/cusp surface of the tooth) of
all teeth since these are fairly small surfaces for which I lack
meaningful information. I do mention relevant facts about these
surfaces where relevant to discussions of the facial or lingual
surfaces.

In total, since I have 28 teeth (with my 4 wisdom teeth having been
removed) and 2 surfaces for each tooth (the facial and lingual
surface) there are a total of 56 facial-cum-lingual tooth surfaces
covered here.

## Lingual surfaces (or more properly, palatal surfaces) of maxillary molars

There are four maxillary molars:

* Left maxillary second molar (adjacent to the now-removed left wisdom tooth): tooth 15
* Right maxillary second molar (adjacent to the now-removed right wisdom tooth): tooth 2
* Left maxillary first molar: tooth 14
* Right maxillary first molar: tooth 3

### Structural features

* The lingual surfaces of maxillary molars are (for the most part)
  clearly convex both mesiodistally and cervico-occlusally. This means
  the brushing challenge with these teeth is that the bulging central
  portion of the convex surface is easy to contact, but the sides less
  so.

* The middle of the lingual surface along the mesiodistal axis have a
  bit of a groove that is known as the lingual groove or palatal
  groove; this runs cervico-occlusally from the root to the tip. In my
  case (and also in general) this is visible for first molars but
  mostly not visible for second molars. This is because first molars
  have more complex crown morphology with more deeply expressed
  developmental grooves, since that explains why rather than just
  describing what.

### Staining and plaque pattern

Generally for me, the lingual surfaces of maxillary molars have most
of their staining and plaque accumulation near the roots, with the
rest of the tooth surface fairly white.

For first molars, there is some accumulation of plaque along with
lingual groove, particularly the root end onward.

## Lingual surfaces (or more properly, palatal surfaces) of maxillary premolars (aka bicuspids)

There are four maxillary premolars (also known as maxillary bicuspids):

* Left maxillary second premolar/bicuspid: tooth 13
* Right maxillary second premolar/bicuspid: tooth 4
* Left maxillary first premolar/bicuspid: tooth 12
* Right maxillary first premolar/bicuspid: tooth 5

### Structural features

The lingual surfaces have a slightly convex profile. The convexity
isn't as significant as for molars, and there are also no major
concavities. This makes them easier to keep clean: the convexity is
slight enough that access to the sides is easy, and the absence of
significant concavity like the lingual grooves means there aren't
hidden areas for plaque to accumulate.

### Staining and plaque pattern

Similar to the molars, the staining and plaque accumulation is mostly
near the roots, maybe a little bit more on the distal side along the
mesiodistal axis. The mesiodistal discrepancy may be due to brushing
stroke directionality as left-handed brusher, tighter interdental
contact on the distal side, or tongue/saliva contact patterns.

## Lingual surfaces (or more properly, palatal surfaces) of maxillary canines (aka cuspids)

There are two maxillary canines (also known as maxillary cuspids):

* Left maxillary canine: tooth 11
* Right maxillary canine: tooth 6

### Structural features

The maxillary canines are generally convex but with a prominent
lingual ridge running cervico-occlusally down the middle, creating two
shallow concavities flanking it. These concavities are harder to reach
my brushing.

The canine tooth lingual surfaces are also somewhat longer than the
surrounding tooth surfaces.

### Staining and plaque pattern

Unlike the molars and premolars, the canines do not show a clear
staining and plaque pattern at the roots. The staining tends to be
away from the root and along parts of the concavities flanking the
ridge on its two sides.

There is also faint staining at the slight concavity near the occlusal
edge.

## Lingual surfaces (or more properly, palatal surfaces) of maxillary incisors

There are four maxillary incisors:

* Left maxillary lateral incisor: tooth 10
* Right maxillary lateral incisor: tooth 7
* Left maxillary central incisor: tooth 9
* Right maxillary central incisor: tooth 8

### Structural features

The surface has a concave zone in the middle (the lingual fossa)
flanked by convex ridges at the margins (the marginal ridges) and a
convex cingulum near the gumline.

Another noteworthy feature visible in photos, which is technically a
feature of the incisal surface and not the lingual surface, but
affects the boundaries of the lingual surface and is captured in
photographs of the lingual surface: mamelons. These are slight
ridges/indentations along the incisal surface.

I have prominent mamelons on my maxillary central incisors (teeth 9
and 8) and somewhat less prominent mamelons on my maxillary lateral
incisors (teeth 10 and 7). The mamelons aren't clearly visible for
mandibular incisors, which is developmentally expected.

The continued visibility of mamelons is evidence against a significant
amount of damage to teeth due to biting and grinding patterns, since
such patterns tend to flatten the mamelons.

### Staining and plaque pattern

Stain accumulation tends to happen at the lingual fossa, away from the
roots.

My 2026-04-29 photos (from just before the [2026-05-01 dental
cleaning](../events/2026/2026-05-01-dental-cleaning.md)) showed
staining throughout the fossa, somewhere from 1/3 along the
cervico-incisal axis to 3/4 along the cervico-incisal axis. The photos
taken 2026-05-17 (about two weeks after the dental cleaning) showed
staining mostly around the 2/3-3/4 area. This is likely because the
dental cleaning was more focused on the cervical end, since that is
closer to the root and poses more plaque risk.

## Experiment design considerations

### Timing as the key variable

The timing of digestive enzymes administration relative to meal is
important because there are two opposing considerations:

* Digestive enzymes at the beginning of the meal may suppress the
  body's own enzyme production, whereas digestive enzymes after meal
  can act on top of the body's enzyme production, creating the most
  additive effect.

* Digestive enzymes at the beginning of the meal provides protection
  right from the beginning, significantly reducing the "unprotected
  window". This is particularly relevant for the digestive enzymes
  that the body doesn't produce in any meaningful quantity at all,
  some of which are a part of the digestive enzymes supplement.

Beyond timing relative to meal, which meal I have the digestive enzyme
with could have some effect: digestive enzymes with the first meal may
be partly available for the second meal as well.

### Gap between administrations

I don't plan to take digestive enzymes regularly, just on occasions
when I have outside food. This means that my consumption of digestive
enzymes is unlikely to cause long-term reduction in enzyme production.

For testing as well, in order to avoid enzyme production suppression
effects, I plan to space out the test administrations by at least
three days (so two days in between with no digestive enzyme).

### Recording protocol

I plan to record the digestive enzymes consumption with `de` (short for
`digestive enzymes`) in Slack self-chat logs, ideally timed to the
exact time of consumption, so that its position relative to the meal
can be calculated.

Beyond that, I expect that my usual Slack self-chat logs of stuff like
bowel movement volume and pattern, bowel pressure pattern after waking
up, and meal times, plus my food preparations and openings logging,
will provide some information that I can use to retrospectively infer
any pattern changes. I will explicitly note any extreme changes, but
otherwise not do any additional logging during execution.

My plan is that, after each phase concludes and before beginning the
next phase, I will document overall results of the phase, and at this
point I will decide what aspects of the information I've collected are
most relevant and how to weight them.

## Phase 2: digestive enzymes in the context of reduced sleep of various kinds

The exact sequence here is to be determined, but the general idea is
to determine the effect of digestive enzymes in scenarios where I have
to wake up much earlier than usual, or sleep much later than usual, so
I don't get the full amount of sleep. Do digestive enzymes help with
bowel movements under these circumstances.

This is harder to design since I want to either pick the reduced-sleep
occasions at a time where they are least likely to disrupt things, or
piggyback on an existing low-sleep situation if I can predict it in
advance.

## Phase 3: digestive enzymes in the context of unusual food consumption (restaurant food, smaller food quantity, larger food quantity)

* The restaurant food case is one of the original motivators. However,
  I'll need to decide what restaurant food to try this with and which
  of the timing variations to use.

* The smaller food quantity case is interesting as a contingency for
  if I am too busy and didn't get a chance to prepare food. In this
  case, I may not have cooked vegetables, though I'll likely still
  have some food like rice, yogurt, almond milk, miso ginger broth,
  sauerkraut, etc. This means a less cellulose-heavy diet, which might
  mean less of the "bulk" pressure. The question is whether digestive
  enzymes can help or hurt with bowel movements here.

* The larger food quantity case is interesting because it means an
  overload on my digestive system, and it would be interesting to see
  if digestive enzymes help by supporting the larger processing
  volume.

# 2026-06-14: floss pick gum injury

## Background

For the past few days, I've noticed that a particular interdental gap,
specifically the gap between teeth 29 and 30 (the right mandibular
premolar/molar gap) has become harder to access with the floss
pick. Multiple attempts and angles are often needed.

## Incident

When flossing with the floss pick / floss handle on 2026-06-14,
shortly past midnight from 2026-06-13, I ended up applying too much
force to get through the gap between teeth 29 and 30. I was not able
to decelerate quickly enough and the floss pick hit the papilla with a
moderate impact, causing a trauma injury, detected by me as blood on
the pick thread and a bit of blood on spitting. The bleeding stopped,
but some sensation of injury and pain persisted.

Somewhat foolishly, I indulged the scientist in myself to try flossing
all interdental gaps three more times. For two of the three rounds, I
was able to access the interdental gap without injury. However, one of
the three rounds led to a similar bleed, perhaps because it poked an
already injured area.

The bleeding had stopped by the time I brushed my teeth shortly
thereafter.

## Analysis

I did some general reading on the Internet and had a [private Claude
conversation](https://claude.ai/chat/0efe2ef9-16a2-4111-97e0-87f6a08ce8d9)
reviewing my experience as well as my learnings from the Internet
reading.

I won't go into all the details of the conversation here, but am
flagging a few salient takeaways:

* Mesial drift and calculus buildup were flagged as potential causes
  of the increased difficulty of penetration.

* It is better to skip an interdental gap than try to use excessive
  force to penetrate it if normal levels of force don't work.

* The use of an angle (with the lingual side more inferior than the
  facial side) and a sawing motion rather than vertical pressure can
  help penetrate the gap without using excessive force and therefore
  without building the speed that can result in a traumatic collision
  with the papilla.

* If the floss pick doesn't work for a particular interdental gap, an
  interdental brush can be used on both the facial and lingual sides
  of the interdental gap. Whereas the floss pick needs to enter
  vertically from the contact point onward (where the teeth are most
  tightly packed against each other), the interdental brush can enter
  from anywhere along the interdental gap, with the most typical entry
  being through the embrasure space (just distal of the
  papilla). Moreover, the interdental brush can partially access the
  surface of the gap even if it fails to fully penetrate t.

  I already do interdental brush cleaning once a day (and floss pick
  cleaning the other time) so in this case I would just be doing it
  twice a day for the gap where I cannot do the floss pick cleaning.

* We also compared the likely peak force that happens in cases like
  the one leading to my injury, against my deliberate floss pick
  pressure testing where I bring the floss pick in contact with the
  papilla and then gradually increase pressure. We determined that the
  latter is much safer, both in terms of lower peak force and in terms
  of gradual ramp-up making the biofeedback work better to both help
  me calibrate force and allow a better local response.

Some general flossing guidance that came up during review:

* Online advice talks about penetrating 1-2 mm into the gumline,
  whereas my own approach is more based on going until I feel clear
  resistance rather than measuring distance. The Claude conversation
  suggests that this is a reasonable proxy.

* Some online advice says to wash the floss thread after each
  interdental gap (before proceeding to the next) to reduce the risk
  of cross-contamination. Review with Claude determined that this was
  probably unnecessary in my case since I've already done a swirl and
  saltwater rinse by the time I get to the floss pick stage, and I
  generally do not find dirt on the floss pick; any particles loosened
  by it come out in my spit rather than adhering to the floss
  pick. However, a recommendation that emerged is that in cases where
  I do see blood or food residue on the floss pick, or after working
  on interdental gaps with known injury or inflammation, a brief wash
  is warranted.

## Aftermath

The flossing the next day worked fine. The interdental gap was still
hard to penetrate, but the angle and sawing motion worked.

The hygienist began by doing scaling with irrigation first, and then

### Examination by dentist

The dentist came to examine the teeth after a few minutes of scaling
by the hygienist. My gums had not reacted well to the scaling so far,
though they were not yet bleeding.

The dentist commented on the inflammation as well, which by this point
had been exacerbated by the initial cleaning. Even though the cleaning
had exacerbated the inflammation, the dentist considered this out of
the norm of what should happen to healthy gums even with the use of
cleaning instruments. In my experience as well, the gums were reacting
worse to the cleaning than they had to prior cleanings.

The dentist also mentioning plaque overall and staining on the back
teeth.

The dentist asked about my brushing and flossing practices. No clear
issues were found, so she suggested just continuing as is, and asked
the hygienist to give me more technique-related tips based on her
findings.

The dentist didn't comment on the recession that she normally comments
on. I don't think the recession got better, so it may be that when
there is inflammation, that gets more focus and there's no comment on
the recession.

### Continued cleaning by hygienist

The hygienist continued to clean. Toward the latter half of the
cleaning, there was a steady stream of slight bleeding, which was
unusual for me and also puzzling to the hygienist.

After the cleaning, the hygienist did the prophy cup polishing. She
also mentioned that she had been more thorough with the cleaning than
usual, because of the greater inflammation.

There was no ozone treatment.

### Tips from hygienist

The hygienist recommended that when flossing, I should jiggle in each
tooth a little more rather than just go in and out, to make sure that

the gum and teeth meet.

## Further reflection and practice updates

The unexpectedly negative results of the dental cleaning left me
pondering what had changed, and what I should do differently going
forward. Some of the improvements going forward may not specifically
target things that changed, but may be good ideas anyway. Others may
be specifically driven by what changed.

### In what ways were things worse?

Before digging into what might have caused things to get worse, it's
helpful to try to articulate whether and how the results were
worse. There are a few concrete ways:

* The evaluation by the dentist and hygienist was worse than usual.

* I experienced more pain and bleeding with the dental cleaning.

Still, it's hard to separate out three ways things could be worse:

* Was there more plaque (and staining)?

* Was there more gum inflammation in general for a comparable level of
  plaque, due to other changes?

* Was the sample time bad for some reason, i.e., things just happened
  to be a bit more inflamed on the date of the dental cleaning?

It's hard to separate out the different hypotheses, because commentary
by the dentist and hygienist can be influenced by what they happen to
notice first. For instance, if they see more inflammation, they may
comment more on the same level of plaque (because it's having worse
effects). It's also hard to separate between a general increase in
inflammation and some factors that caused increased inflammation on
that specific day.

Overall, the margin of difference, while seemingly big for me, is
still within the gingivitis range and not getting into the
periodontitis range.

Fortunately, I do have self-photos (something that I wasn't sure I
should spend time on given the overall time crunch, but in hindsight
I'm glad I took).

#### Pressure testing as an indicator but also a confounding cause

The one thing I remember finding a bit different when I did the dental
self-exam on 2025-10-21 was the results of pressure testing. Here are my notes:

> I experienced a little bit of pain for a few seconds from the use of
> the floss pick when pressed hard against the gums. The pain was more
> in some areas than others -- the upper right and upper middle had
> somewhat sharper pain. Overall, the pain subsided within a few
> seconds. The pushing actions did not cause pain

The notes don't make it super-clear but my subjective sense is that
this was notable worse than 2025-04-14.

Interestingly, this was my first time noticing any gum pain symptoms,
and it was the day before the dental cleaning and a direct result of
pressure testing!

This suggests that the pressure testing might be a good gauge of gum
inflammation and gum health issues. But, it also raises the
possibility that the pressure testing itself might be creating an
effect of inflaming the gums more for a day or two, and that might
have contributed to the inflammation noticed during dental
cleaning. The last two times, I did the dental self-exam checklist
*two days* prior to the dental cleaning, rather than one day prior, so
there was more scope for healing from the pressure testing.

Going forward, my plan is to make sure to do the dental self-exam
checklist every two months, but also to make sure that I execute the
denatl self-exam checklist about a week prior to the dental
cleaning. In particular, the pressure testing portion should be done a
week before the dental cleaning, so that any short-term effects of the
pressure testing are fully reversed by the time of the cleaning. The
margin of one week also allows me to take any additional actions (such
as a more informed decision regarding whether to pause sauerkraut and
walnuts).

My overall take is that there's weak evidence supporting the idea of

### Ways my dental practices changed (for better and worse) and how those might have contributed

I made a few deliberate changes to my dental practices. There were
also some drifts in practices, particularly as relates to timing and
technique, though it's hard to clearly say whether and how much they
got worse. Let's break it down.

#### Switch to ultra soft toothbrush: potentially causal in making things worse

As documented
[here](2025-04-01-use-of-perio-toothbrush-and-baking-soda-toothpaste.md),
I switched from a soft toothbrush to an ultra soft toothbrush on
2025-04-01. This was about two weeks prior to the previous dental
cleaning on 2025-04-16, so the 2025-10-22 dental cleaning is the first
dental cleaning where I was on an ultra soft toothbrush throughout;
the two weeks for the previous dental cleaning don't count.

My reasoning for switching to the ultra soft toothbrush was that it'll
be better at preventing further recession; at the time, recession was
the primary consideration as gum inflammation and plaque seemed to be
under control.

However, the ultra soft toothbrush might be just that little bit less
powerful at sweeping out the plaque, and an exposure over 6 months
might be enough to make things add up.

For now, my thinking about this is:

* I want to stick to the ultra soft toothbrush for a little longer,
  but take other steps to compensate in the areas with more plaque
  accumulation. I hope to get the best of both worlds: sensitive in
  the areas with recession potential, while compensating with more
  thoroughness in areas with more plaque accumulation.

* I may revisit my thinking based on the results of frequent mirror
  checks and 2-month dental self-exam checklist execution.

#### Occasional use of baking soda toothpaste: likely too infrequent to make a meaningful difference

Along with my switch to an ultra soft toothbrush on 2025-04-01, I also
started occasionally using a baking soda toothpaste on my back
teeth. The total number of times between the cleanings I used the
baking soda on my back teeth was about 30 (this was at the lower end
of the range I was targeting).

Based on the staining photos, my guess is that the frequency of
this use was too low to meaningfully make a difference. My plan is to
continue doing this as I don't think it is hurting anything, and
perhaps increase the frequency, but also understand that it's not very
effective even at a somewhat increased frequency.

#### Drift in timing of flossing and rinsing: potentially made things worse

I have generally tried to do flossing and rinsing at the end of
post-meal cleanup, which works out to being about 30 to 60 minutes
after the end of the meal. My current understanding is that about 30
to 60 minutes after the end of the meal is ideal for flossing and
rinsing -- earlier might be a bit too soon, and later might be a bit
too late.

However, in the past few months, I have adopted a practice of
splitting post-meal cleanup into two rounds, with the first round
focused on putting food in the fridge, then a break to check things on
computer and log stuff related to preparations and openings, and then
a second round, usually multiplexed with listening to video and audio
content using my bone conduction headphones.

With this two-round structure, there is a gap in between that delays
the end of post-meal cleanup. While this gap is generally about 10 to
20 minutes, there have been many times over the past few months when
the gap stretches to 30 minutes or more.

On top of that, I have also tried to do more stuff within the bucket
of post-meal cleanup, rather than delaying them to later (as I used to
do).

The net effect is that rinsing and flossing were often getting
delayed to well over an hour after the meal.

This is not a sudden big jump (unlike the toothbrush switch) but more
of a drift over time, with a lot of fluctuation.

#### Drift in technique of flossing, specifically particular missed interdental gaps: potentially made things worse

I think I might over time have developed blind spots of missing
specific interdental gaps when flossing. Not that I miss a specific
interdental gap all the time, but I might be missing a specific
interdental gap like 30% of the time. The specific ones that I think I
have a high risk of missing are the medial interdental gap and the rightmost and
leftmost interdental gaps.

With the middle teeth, the issue is losing track when I switch the
orientation of my handle to transition from teeth on one side to teeth
on the other side.

With the rightmost and leftmost interdental gap (the one between the
two extreme molars) the issue is that it's easy to miss these when
probing around for interdental gaps.

There's some anecdotal evidence, for instance, from the pressure
testing and bleeding patterns, that the middle and rightmost teeth
were among the ones presenting the most problems. With that said, the
problems weren't super-localized, so I don't think this is the whole
story.

While I don't have good data to suggest that my frequency of missing
particular teeth has gone up over time, I do think I was in general
more conscious and thoughtful about the area around the wisdom teeth in
the immediate aftermath of wisdom tooth removal, and in general my
flossing in more recent months has been more on autopilot. In general,
I've taken the attitude that even if I miss a particular interdental
gap once, it's okay because I'll get it next time. But further
reflection suggests that there are particular biases and blind spots
that cause specific gaps to get missed often enough that it
meaningfully affects the cleanliness of those areas.

#### Skipping of dental self-exam checklist: potentially made things worse by not letting me discover the issue sooner

Due to being busy, I didn't execute the dental self-exam checklist
after the dental cleaning or at the 2-month or 4-month mark. This
means that I didn't get early feedback on the situation. I'm not sure
how much that feedback would have changed my trajectory, or if I would
even have noticed any issue at all. But at least combined with my
improved understanding, I expect that sticking to regular execution of
the checklists will be helpful.

#### Differences in protocols shortly before the dental cleaning: potentially made things worse in terms of sample timing

Two things come to mind:

* The pressure testing for the dental self-exam checklist that
  happened the day before the cleaning may have exacerbated the
  inflammation. Going forward, I plan to do the dental self-exam
  checklist a week prior to the dental cleaning.

* I aborted my pause of sauerkraut and walnuts, so I was having these
  right till the day before the dental cleaning. So, if sauerkraut and
  walnuts exacerbate the inflammation, then I might have been looking
  artificially better in past cleanings where I paused these for a few
  days before the dental cleaning. If this is a meaningful
  contributor, then this time's feedback may be more reflective of the
  real situation (though it may still be wise to pause sauerkraut a
  little longer just so the dental cleaning experience is not too
  bad).

# 2019-08-30: Thai yellow curry and subsequent runny nose and throat irritation

## Original incident

Written: September 7, 2019

On Thursday, August 29, 2019, the Chaat Cafe, an Indian restaurant
that I've been getting food from about thrice a week, shut down.

To reinstate some dietary diversity, on August 30, 2019, I got a
yellow curry from Bangkok Thai Cuisine. This is a typical Thai yellow
curry, with carrots, potatoes, and onions.

Starting Saturday, August 31, I had a runny nose and thoat irritation
for 2-3 days. I believe this is because the yellow curry was quite
heavy. However, I can't rule out the possibility of an infection.

The runny nose peaked on the weekend and was mostly recovered by
Monday. Tuesday it was almost completely recovered. Wednesday was a
normal day.

I believe it was an irritation caused by the yellow curry and not
an infection, because normally infections need more recovery time.

I tried the Thai yellow curry again on Friday September 6. This time,
I made a few changes:

* I didn't have the whole yellow curry -- only about half of it.

* I mixed it with my cooked meal (tomatoes, eggplants, bell peppers)
  to reduce the relative overdose of the spices in the yellow curry
  and also with almond milk.

I felt a somewhat similar irritation, but lesser in magnitude, so I
believe this dosage works well. There was no runny nose the next day
(Saturday, September 7).

I'll have the remaining half of the yellow curry after a few days.

## Retrospective notes written May 2026

In May 2026, as part of a more systematic review of various restaurant
foods in a [Claude
conversation](https://claude.ai/chat/01e356aa-a7fd-495e-9fda-81ddc37b385a),
and in particular, in light of trying the yellow curry again after a
gap of several years, I revisited some of the themes here. Below are
some insights I gleaned:

* The combination of warm throat soreness plus runny nose as a package
  is a fairly clean curcumin signature. Turmeric, and more
  specifically the curcumin component of it, activates the TRPA1 and
  TRPV1 receptors, but it's different from capsaicin (the
  spice/hotness ingredient) in that it produces a diffuse warming
  sensation rather than a hot burn. According to Claude: "The
  combination of warm throat soreness plus runny nose as a package is
  a fairly clean curcumin signature."

* Thai yellow curry is rich in turmeric, much more so than my
  home-cooked meals that do have turmeric but bloomed in oil and mixed
  over a much larger stew.

* Not only does Thai yellow curry have a higher concentration of
  turmeric; the coconut milk base used in the yellow curry increases
  curcumin bioavailability compared to the more water-based mixed
  vegetable stew. This is similar to the mechanism of using black
  pepper to increase curcumin absorption.

* Beyond curcumin, other ingredients that could be contributing to the
  TRPA1 and TRPV1 activation are: onions, coconut milk itself, and soy
  (tofu).

* Dilution with almond milk and vegan yogurt somewhat reverses things
  by reducing the contact dose of curcumin at the TRPA1 and TRPV1
  receptors. Almond milk operates primarily by dilution; yogurt has an
  additional viscosity-based effect.

It's important to note a key distinction between turmeric and
cumin. Both turmeric and cumin can lead to a more runny nose, but
turmeric's primary direct effect is a secretagogue effect of more
mucus production, whereas cumin has a stronger decongestant effect
through bronchodilation. Both may produce a more runny nose in the
short run, but whereas turmeric is doing so by producing more mucus,
cumin is doing so by allowing easier release of existing mucus. That's
why cumin is recommended for sore throats and colds, while turmeric is
not.

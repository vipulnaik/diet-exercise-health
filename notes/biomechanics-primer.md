# Biomechanics primer

This document is intended as a basic primer on biomechanics that
serves to frame common themes to be referenced in my various physical
exercise documents. It doesn't go into detail but provides enough
reference hooks for things that can be looked up.

## Force and acceleration concepts

### Force and acceleration metrics

We have:

* Force
* Acceleration (force per unit mass)
* Yank (rate of change of force over time)
* Jerk (rate of change of acceleration over time)
* Impulse (integral of force over time, important for the case of very large forces), can also be thought of as the before/after change in momentum

### Units anchored around gravity

Typical units are anchored around g (acceleration due to gravity at
the surface of the earth), a useful comparison because most of the
time, when stationary on the surface of earth, we feel the
acceleration due to gravity as the force on us.

* Force: G (force due to gravity; this is not a unit of force because
  it depends on the mass, but it's often used because what's
  determined from a physical context is not the force itself but the
  acceleration it is trying to produce)

* Acceleration: g

* Yank: G/s (G per second)

* Jerk: g/s (g per second)

* Impulse: Gs (G times second)

### Direction

In the biomechanical context, coordinates work as follows (see, e.g.,
[this](https://www.faa.gov/pilots/safety/pilotsafetybrochures/Acceleration.pdf))
where we keep in mind that we are talking about the contact force
(pseudo-force) experienced by the body relative to the object it is
moving in, rather than the force as experienced by the vehicle from
earth's vantage point, which is opposite.

* Gx refers to acceleration in the direction connecting front to back.

  * Positive multiple of Gx is chest-to-back or eyeballs-in. For
    instance, the acceleration we experience when facing forward in an
    accelerating vehicle, so we are pushed chest-to-back or
    eyeballs-in.

  * Negative multiple of Gx is back-to-chest or eyeballs-out. For
    instance, the acceleration we experience when facing forward in a
    braking vehicle, back-to-chest or eyeballs out.

  Note that when facing backward (as we might in some seats of a bus
  or train) the associations reverse.

* Gy refers to the lateral direction.

  * Positive multiple of Gy is right-to-left or eyeballs-left. This is
    the acceleration we experience when facing forward in a vehicle
    turning right.

  * Negative multiple of Gy is left-to-right or eyeballs-right. This is
    the acceleration we experience when facing forward in a vehicle
    turning left.

  Note that when facing backward (as we might in some seats of a bus
  or train) the associations reverse.

* Gz refers to the vertical direction between head and foot.

  * Positive multiple of Gz is head-to-foot. For instance, the
    acceleration we experience in an elevator when it accelerates up
    (starting upward journey) or decelerates down (ending downward
    journey), so we are pushed head-to-foot. Essentially, we feel
    heavier.

  * Negative multiple of Gz is foot-to-head. For instance, the
    acceleration we experience in an elevator when it accelerates down
    (starting downward journey) or decelerates up (ending upward
    journey) so we are pushed foot-to-head. When less in magnitude
    than G, we feel lighter, like we are floating or falling.

We can therefore talk of somebody subject to 0.2 Gx, which could occur
if they are facing forward in a vehicle accelerating at 0.2 times the
acceleration due to gravity. Or being subject to -0.3 Gx, which would
occur if they are facing forward in a vehicle decelerating at 0.3
times the acceleration due to gravity.

## Production, power, and tolerance

It's useful to make a distinction between force we produce and force
we tolerate.

### Force production

Force production involves neuromuscular coordination to produce force
of our own volition, expending our own energy as needed.

Force production can be in a static / isometric context (holding
something in position, with no movement) or a dynamic / kinetic
context (moving something).

The extent of force production that can be summoned for a particular
purpose is highly sensitive to the structure and purpose. For
instance, it can vary between static and dynamic force production, it
can vary between the left hand and the right hand, it can vary based
on the shape of the hold or the direction of application. It's a
complicated balance of neuromuscular, metabolic, and structural
factors.

### Mechanical and metabolic power

The mechanical power output of force production is the power it
creates as force times velocity. This is zero for static / isometric
force production (e.g., holding something still) and positive for
dynamic / kinetic force production.

The metabolic power spent in force production is the metabolic energy
spent per unit time while producing the force.

For metabolic power, the question of what the right baseline is
becomes important, because we ideally want to subtract out the
baseline of how much power one would have spent otherwise. There are
various options: BMR, RMR, position-matched baseline. Unfortunately,
for many local exercises (like hand grippers) the incremental power is
very small and hard to distinguish from overall metabolic rate
fluctuations.

### Tolerance (more structural)

Tolerance refers to the force that can be tolerated without structural
damage. Generally, the maximum force production falls well short of
the tolerance (ranging from 50% to 80%, going higher usually in
emergency situations with adrenaline pumping); this is the
neuromuscular safety buffer to prevent injury. Tolerance becomes
relevant when passively subject to force, e.g., being in an
accelerating or decelerating vehicle, falling, being hit by an object.

Tolerance is more structural whereas force production is more
neuromuscular. In particular, the central player in tolerance is the
bones (with muscle and fat playing a padding role), whereas the
central player in force production is the neuromuscular
system. Obviously, the two go hand in hand. Given that force
production is capped relative to tolerance, we have to improve
tolerance in order to push force production beyond some point.

Completely passive tolerance is one end of the spectrum: this is when
you fall while unconscious (an extreme of a syncope fall). Here, it's
entirely structural: bone mineral density and structure, with muscle
and fat padding helping cushion the fall.

When you fall while still conscious, you're not completely doing
passive tolerance: you can engage in muscle activation, change the
direction of fall, and tense muscles appropriately. So, strength
matters more.

## Where exercise fits

Exercise most directly improves force production and mechanical power
output, increasing both peak values of force production and mechanical
power output as well as the integral of these over time. Each of these
(peak force production, peak mechanical power output, total force
production over time, total mechanical power output over time aka
mechanical energy produced) is a somewhat different thing and is
optimized by somewhat different exercises. For instance, for peak
force production, training with heavy weights matters more.

To add to the complexity, the training also differs by the organ and
the specific shape of the problem (e.g., peak force production in a
dynamic context may not translate to peak force production in a static
context).

Exercise also improves passive tolerance, mediated through bone
strength and extra padding gained through hypertrophy. Interestingly,
passive alternatives to exercise (where loads are imposed on one
without force production, for instance, roller coaster rides) can also
in principle give some of the passive tolerance improvement benefits
while given very few force production and metabolic power benefits.

Exercise, when done right, also improves active tolerance, combining
the passive tolerance increases with the strength to effectively make
use of muscle activation e.g., when falling with some degree of
preparation. However, these benefits are only unlocked if reflexes are
fast enough to trigger the muscle activation.

### Note on jerk production and tolerance

Jerk is interesting because it's not often talked about in exercise
training, but it's very relevant to accidents or unexpected contact.

Humans are pretty good at jerk production -- walking and running
involves a decent amout of jerk, and several movements involve
jerk. Some exercises -- particularly explosive movement exercises --
can make one even better at jerk production.

Humans are naturally not that great at passive tolerance of
jerk. They're a little better at active tolerance of jerk. Note that
the 50 ms reaction time floor means that the first 50 ms or so of jerk
response are passive.
